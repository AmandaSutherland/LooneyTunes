Makeover of the Bow

We've had quite some problem with our bow ever since our first four-string prototype assembly. The wood material did not work as well as we thought it would, because of the very uneven contact surfaces between the strings and the bow, and inconsistant friction with the strings. We then decided to glue some horsehair onto the wooden bow wheel to see if there's any improvement. However, sadly, the result was a hot mess. We had trouble getting the horsehair onto the wheel in a very orderly and well distributed way. Over time the horsehair also began to fall off at places where there were no glue. Therefore gluing horsehair onto a wooden wheel turned out to be not a very good solution.

We then turned to an acrylic wheel, because we believed that the contact surface would be much smoother from the laser cutting machine. However, turns out that the acrylic wheel was also not a viable solution. We then wondered if attaching strips of horsehair to the surface of a rubber strip would help with the smoothness and distribution of the horsehair. The rubber also sticks better onto the surface of the acrylic wheel, as hair and plastic do not stick well to each other.

Finally we figured out the perfect plan to create this bow. We braided the horsehair and cut groves into the acrylic wheel to position the hair at opposite ends of the wheel. This way none of the hair has to be glued down, we have maximum contact between the bow and the strings, and we have good amount of friction between the horsehair and the strings.

Decision to Move to Arduino Mega

Today is a sad day for Team Looney Tunes. For around the 5th time, the Beagle Bone has crashed while we were trying to integrate it with the entire system. It works with each individual part, but freezes when asked to perform complex tasks. We've had to re-flash it several times in order to resolve it being frozen. As shown in the image below, sometimes the microprocessor will freeze with only two lights on. (Normal operation is with all lights blinking.) Overall, although the Beagle Bone's features are perfect for what we want to do, it is not reliable enough for our robotic violin. We have switched to the Arduino Mega. This microprocessor will not connect to the web as we wanted to, but will reliably work.

Final Prototype Assembly

Since Thanksgiving Break, we have been working on our final prototype. We fixed things we didn't like and things that were problematic from the previous prototype. These changes included obtaining nice flat wood, adding more support to decrease the bending of the bridge, designing a new encoder mounting, and adding secondary encoders.

Once we've compiled all of our parts for our entire system, we began our assembly process. In the meantime, we also tested our motors with the Beagle Bone.

Critical Design Review - 11.19.13

The goal for this design review was to make sure we had all of our critical subsystems working, or at least we have an idea of how to solve the critical problems.

Our critical subsystems included: transition from 1 string to 4 strings, addition of an amplifier, making sure the "string pulling" system works, and finally having all connections through the Beagle Bone. We were able to have all of our critical subsystems ready, except for the connection through the Beagle Bone. We believed that our bow wheel was not a critical subsystem.

Preliminary Design Review - 10.28.13

For this preliminary design review, we talked about our design goals, our three main mechanical, electrical, and software subsystems, and our schedule for this final project. Some of our goals include: we will create a string instrument that is controlled and played mechanically, the instrument will be capable of playing music that a traditional violin cannot play (e.g. chords and large interval gaps), and the system will connect to an engaging interface for listerners to choose songs from.

Before throwing ourselves into working on the three separate subsystems, some major design decisions had to be made. We decided that we would control the pitch of the strings by means of changing the tension of the string, instead of having set pressers to press down on the strings to change the pitch. We also decided to use a BeagleBone as our microcontroller because of the vast amount of pins it has.

From all these decisions and subsystems, we've decided that the most critical part of our system is being able to change the pitch of the string by means of changing the tension of the string. Hence for our Preliminary Design Review, we made sure to have a working tension system to showcase. Below attached is our PowerPoint of our presentation for the design review.
